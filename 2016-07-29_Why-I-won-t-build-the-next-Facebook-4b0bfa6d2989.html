Why I won’t build the next Facebook

And why you shouldn’t either

Why I won’t build the next Facebook

We’re all entrepreneurs now. We are entering (or indeed as some would argue — have entered) a world where anyone can decide to do anything. Allow me to label this as our current culture; we’ve given ourselves the green light on seeing our of ideas through from conception to actualisation. However, I’m worried it’s a culture of heavily, almost solely, tech-centred innovation.

Here’s the issue, it’s too predictable.

A hip young team builds yet another app that totally disrupts another industry (à la Uber), or keeps you coming back for some deliciously crappy gameplay (think Flappy Bird). Sure, they’re changing the world, but only digitally.

When one looks at real things being built, it is only smartphones and LEDs that have truly revolutionised our lives to a mentionable degree, the medium through which you’re reading this now proving as much. Despite those, what tangibles have we produced since then that would change other’s lives in a similar vein?

We’re so busy harnessing the internet that we’re missing some of the underlying problems our world still faces. The reality is simple; not everyone has food on their plate and a shelter over their head. And yet, we nearly have cars that drive themselves and rockets that land the way they left?

Innovation is our modern buzzword, but it needs to mean something different.

I want to talk about what, as a 19-year-old, innovation really means for a younger generation’s future, or what it has the potential to mean.

Having taken some time off from university, I’ve been working in the field of technology innovation to help produce (funnily enough) self-driving cars, amongst other things. This experience has brought to light that if we continue the way we’re going, sure — Google will give internet connection to tens of millions of people across rural India using a balloon, but are these creatives funnelling their energy into solving the food crisis with the same vigour?

Of course, Google’s work is brilliantly imaginative and not without impressive purpose, but I worry it is void of the intensity of innovation in social change that we require today. It makes the market for innovation intended for use by people like you and me, however, appear saturated. I don’t claim to be an innovator for social change, nor do I yet boast the skills to be one, but I do hope that I can inspire a direct a flow of creative minds toward it.

My goal is to excite purpose in young people and to help found a desire to create new forms of change.

Everyone is born with an innate set of gifts that is unique to them; the more people I meet, the more people I can try and bring together to use those gifts to create real beneficial change. One of the most valuable steps for us as a generation is to actually start giving a sh*t about what we build, and why we build it. With this approach, I am confident we can induce the need for change. Indeed, it is this give-a-damn attitude that will begin to define the value of our next “culture” of companies in a way that no amount of discounted cash-flow analyses, revenue stream calculations or price-to-earnings ratios ever can. The concept is not a complicated one. Our new culture of innovators should continue to flip the world off and do things their own way, but do it in the way that helps the person next to them too.

Now is a time, more than ever before, where the opportunity to build tangible things that feed basic necessities is massive.

Nevertheless, my vision extends beyond only the field of those starting their own companies. When Apple Inc. decides to put environmental conservation at their forefront of their agenda, it’s beneficial not only the planet but also to their own balance sheets, lowering their costs as a result of eco-friendly initiatives. Cultivating a culture of questioning how big businesses can help solve today’s problems is as valuable as establishing new enterprises that practice that mentality from the get-go.

“Where is the world headed? Wherever we take it ... we’re the entrepreneurs.” — Casey Gerald

This is my personal pledge to start a journey in hopes of inspiring change. It’s an oppurtunity to prototype my content by posting regularly, and hopefully create something meaningful while I’m at it.
